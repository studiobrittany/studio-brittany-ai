# PINTEREST KEYWORD RESEARCH

Pinterest is a visual search engine, not a social feed. That framing matters for keyword research specifically: people search Pinterest the way they search Google, by typing what they're looking for, not by following accounts the way they would on Instagram or TikTok. Run this file's process for a real keyword research pass: a new content pillar, a board strategy, or a competitor gap check. For a fast keyword check on a single pin, the lighter version of this process (autocomplete plus Guided Search tiles) is usually enough.

---

## 1. WHY PINTEREST KEYWORDS AREN'T GOOGLE KEYWORDS

Pinterest search phrasing skews more visual, more aspirational, and more discovery-phrased than Google. Someone Googles "best project management app." The same person Pinterest-searches "aesthetic notion dashboard ideas." Same underlying need, completely different words. Never port a Google keyword list onto Pinterest assuming it'll perform the same. Always pull from Pinterest's own search behavior.

**The unbranded-search reality, confirmed via Pinterest's own Business page (business.pinterest.com/insights/effective-reach/) as of June 2026:** roughly 96 to 97% of Pinterest searches are unbranded (the exact figure cited varies slightly by source and by whether "top searches" or "all searches" is being measured, but every source converges in the 96 to 97% range). Practically, this means broad, discovery-phrased keywords reach a much bigger pool of people than branded terms. Most Pinterest users aren't searching for a company name; they're searching for an idea, a fix, or an aesthetic.

## 2. SEARCH INTENT ON PINTEREST SPECIFICALLY

| Intent | Looks like | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Inspirational / informational | Browsing ideas, no purchase yet | "aesthetic content calendar ideas" |
| Commercial investigation | Comparing before a decision | "best notion template for content creators" |
| Transactional | Ready to click through and get it | "notion content planner template" |

Pinterest skews more top-of-funnel than Google overall (more browsing, more planning-ahead behavior), but transactional searches absolutely happen. Pinterest's own positioning leans into "shopping with intent," and the platform has built shopping-specific surfaces accordingly.

## 3. NATIVE FREE PINTEREST TOOLS

**Search bar autocomplete:** type a seed term into Pinterest's own search bar (logged out/incognito for unbiased results) and note what autocompletes. This is Pinterest's own language for the topic and the single best free source.

**Guided Search tiles:** the colored keyword tiles Pinterest surfaces below the search bar after an initial search. Click or tap through a few rounds of these tiles to surface related terms Pinterest itself considers connected. This is essentially Pinterest handing over its own related-keyword map for free.

**Pinterest Trends** (trends.pinterest.com): free, built into every Pinterest business account, confirmed live and active as of June 2026. Lets you compare multiple terms for trend direction, includes a keyword-comparison view, and (when account region is set to United States, Canada, or United Kingdom) a "Shopping Trends" view that separates general search-intent volume from shopping-intent volume, with product-level rankings and demographic detail. Pinterest has stated it plans to expand Shopping Trends to more regions over time. Useful for telling the difference between "people are curious about this" and "people are about to buy this."

**Pinterest Ads Manager keyword targeting path:** under Create Campaign, Traffic objective, Targeting, Pinterest's ad keyword search still surfaces keyword suggestions even without spending money (cancel before checkout). Useful for keyword discovery. The caveat: as of 2026, Pinterest only selectively shows actual search-volume numbers through this path. A keyword showing no number doesn't mean no one searches it; it means Pinterest isn't surfacing that number right now. Use this path for keyword discovery, not for volume data.

## 4. THIRD-PARTY PINTEREST TOOLS

**KeywordToPin** (keywordtopin.com): an all-in-one Pinterest keyword research, analytics, and marketing tool. Core features: a Hidden Keyword Discovery Tool that surfaces keywords Pinterest doesn't expose through normal search, competitor analysis, trending pin/keyword discovery, performance analytics, bulk pin uploads, and auto-posting.

Pricing as confirmed directly at keywordtopin.com in June 2026: three tiers, Starter at $25/month, Professional at $35/month, and Expert at $45/month, with a 14-day free trial and no credit card required to start. Confirm current pricing at the source before it goes into a budget conversation, since SaaS pricing changes without notice industry-wide.

**Tailwind's Pinterest keyword tool:** a free keyword finder that generates keyword suggestions from a website URL, with no account required. Worth checking first if Tailwind already handles pin scheduling in the workflow, since it's free and already part of a tool being opened regularly.

**PinClicks** (pinclicks.com): Pinterest-specific tool with rank tracking over time and a large interest/keyword database. Pricing confirmed directly at pinclicks.com/pricing in June 2026: Pin Pro at $29/month, Pin Plus at $49/month, with a 5-day free trial and no credit card required. Annual billing offers a meaningful discount over monthly.

**Pin Inspector:** desktop tool, one-time purchase rather than a subscription. Pricing confirmed at $67 for lifetime access as of June 2026, including free software updates. Good fit for anyone who'd rather own a tool outright instead of adding another monthly subscription.

None of these tools need to replace each other. Pick based on budget and workflow: a free option (Pinterest's own tools, Tailwind's keyword finder) is enough for most early-stage research; a paid subscription (KeywordToPin, PinClicks) adds depth and ongoing tracking; a one-time purchase (Pin Inspector) suits anyone who wants to avoid recurring costs.

## 5. KEYWORD PLACEMENT CHECKLIST

Once keywords are researched, they go in (in priority order):
1. Profile name/bio
2. Board titles and board descriptions
3. Pin titles (front-loaded; the first word matters most)
4. Pin descriptions (front-loaded in the first 50 to 60 characters)
5. Image file name before upload (Pinterest reads this)
6. Alt text

This checklist covers where researched keywords land. Exact pin-copy templates and character-count formatting are a separate, copywriting-stage task outside the scope of keyword research itself.

## 6. KEYWORD BANK ORGANIZATION

Keep one running keyword bank per content pillar or product line rather than starting from zero on every pin. A simple structure: seed keyword, then 3 to 5 validated variants, intent tag, which board/pillar it serves, and last-checked date (Pinterest search behavior shifts seasonally, so a bank older than a few months is worth a re-check). This bank fits naturally into whatever tool is already part of a regular workflow: a spreadsheet, a Notion database, a project management tool, or even a plain document. The specific tool matters far less than actually revisiting it; a keyword bank nobody reopens is dead weight.

## 7. WORKFLOW

1. Pull seeds from the product, post, or pillar itself, stated in the searcher's own language
2. Run seeds through Pinterest search bar autocomplete and Guided Search tiles (free, do this every time)
3. Cross-check in Pinterest Trends for direction and, where available, the Shopping Trends split
4. Layer in a third-party tool for hidden-keyword and trending-pin data where deeper research is warranted
5. Tag each keyword's intent (inspirational, commercial investigation, transactional)
6. Score and prioritize using the 4-axis method in `core-keyword-fundamentals.md`
7. Hand off the finished, validated keyword set to the pin-copywriting step, or log it in the keyword bank for future use

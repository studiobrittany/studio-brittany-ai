# BLOG & WEBSITE SEO KEYWORD RESEARCH

Use this process for any blog or website keyword research that's bigger than a single post: a new content pillar, a quarterly content calendar, a cluster map, or a competitor gap analysis. For a single post, the lighter version (one focus keyword, a handful of secondary keywords) is usually enough.

---

## 1. MACRO VS. MICRO RESEARCH

**Macro research** happens before a content calendar or pillar gets planned. The output is a cluster map: one parent topic, multiple supporting posts, organized so they interlink instead of compete with each other for the same search intent.

**Micro research** happens before a single post gets written. The output is one focus keyword plus a short list of secondary keywords for that specific piece. Use the lighter, single-post version of this process directly for that, and reach for the full macro process when the post is part of a bigger pillar or cluster decision.

## 2. FOCUS KEYWORD + SECONDARY KEYWORDS

Every post needs exactly one focus keyword (the phrase the post is built to win) and 3 to 6 secondary keywords (supporting variants and related terms it can also realistically rank for). This is the same model Yoast SEO's keyphrase field is built around, and it's become an industry-standard structure for a reason: one clear target keeps a post from trying to be everything at once and ranking for nothing.

## 3. KEYWORD PLACEMENT

Once the focus and secondary keywords are set, they belong in: title tag, meta description, URL slug, H1, at least one H2, the first 100 words of body copy, naturally throughout the body (not stuffed), and image alt text. Typical density targets cited across the industry run roughly 1 to 2% for the primary keyword and 0.5 to 1% for secondary keywords, though modern search engines weight natural, query-answering phrasing far more heavily than exact density. This file covers deciding which keywords earn that placement in the first place; exact density and placement enforcement is an execution-stage task.

## 4. COMPETITOR GAP ANALYSIS

The fastest way to find a real opportunity: pull the top 5 ranking pages for the target topic and note what subtopics, questions, or angles they all cover that the site in question doesn't touch yet. Paid tools (Ahrefs' Content Gap, Semrush's Keyword Gap) automate this by comparing domains directly. Without that budget, the manual version works: read the top 5 results, list every H2/H3 they share, and treat anything missing from the existing content as the gap to fill.

## 5. THE METRICS, APPLIED PRACTICALLY

See `core-keyword-fundamentals.md` Section 5 for the full six-metric breakdown. For blog/website work specifically:
- **Traffic Potential** matters more than raw Search Volume when deciding what to build as a pillar page. A pillar's value comes from everything it can rank for, not just its named keyword
- **Keyword Difficulty** is the hard ceiling for a smaller site. Without backlinks already in place, anything in the "hard" or "very hard" band (roughly KD 31+, see `core-keyword-fundamentals.md` Section 5 for the full breakdown) is a long game, not a quick win
- **Business Potential** should outrank Search Volume in any tiebreak. A lower-volume keyword that's two clicks from a sale beats a high-volume keyword that's purely informational with no path to a product

## 6. WHY THIS MATTERS: THE ZERO-TRAFFIC REALITY

Worth knowing before sinking real time into the wrong keywords: Ahrefs' original large-scale Content Explorer study found that 90.63% of pages get zero organic search traffic from Google. A newer, larger Ahrefs study (roughly 14 billion pages from their Content Explorer index) found the figure even higher, at 96.55% (ahrefs.com/blog/search-traffic-study/). Both numbers are legitimately Ahrefs-sourced, just from different study sizes and years; Ahrefs itself notes the more recent figure isn't a perfectly representative sample of the entire web, but it's the most-cited version of this finding in the SEO industry as of 2026. The headline point holds either way: most published content gets found by nobody, and the deciding factor is almost always whether the keyword work happened before the writing did, not after.

## 7. PRIORITIZATION

Use the same 4-axis scoring system from `core-keyword-fundamentals.md` Section 6 (relevance, volume, winnability, business potential, each 1 to 5). For a content calendar, rank every candidate keyword this way before slotting it into the calendar. It turns "what should I write about next" into a ranked list instead of a guess.

## 8. COMMON MISTAKES SPECIFIC TO BLOG/WEBSITE SEO

- Writing the post first and finding keywords after: backwards every time
- One post per keyword with no clustering, leaving the site fighting itself for the same intent across multiple pages
- Chasing a single huge head-term keyword instead of a cluster of realistic long-tail variants that add up to more total traffic
- Ignoring search intent mismatch: ranking page 1 for a keyword whose intent doesn't match the page's actual content still won't convert
- Skipping the competitor gap check and re-covering ground other sites already own

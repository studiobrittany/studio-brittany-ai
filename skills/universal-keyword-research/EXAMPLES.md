# EXAMPLES

Nine worked scenarios showing this skill's process applied to different content types. Each example includes the starting context, the steps actually taken, and the resulting output.

**A note on the numbers in this file:** search volume, keyword difficulty, and traffic figures shown below are illustrative placeholder numbers used to demonstrate the workflow and output format. They are not live search data. Before using any number in a real deliverable, pull the current figure from an actual tool (see `references/core-keyword-fundamentals.md` Section 8) or platform-native source, per the validation rule in Section 7 of that same file.

## Example 1: Pinterest content pillar for a new product line

**Context:** A creator sells digital budget planners and wants to build a Pinterest content pillar around a new "debt payoff tracker" product before launch.

**Steps taken:**
1. Seed keywords pulled from the product itself: "debt payoff tracker," "debt snowball," "debt free journey"
2. Ran each seed through Pinterest's search bar (logged out) and Guided Search tiles
3. Cross-checked in Pinterest Trends, region set to US, with Shopping Trends enabled
4. Tagged intent per result
5. Scored and ranked using the 4-axis system

**Output:**

| Keyword | Intent | Relevance | Volume | Winnability | Biz potential | Total | Status |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| debt snowball tracker printable | Transactional | 5 | 4 | 4 | 5 | 18 | Validated (autocomplete + Trends) |
| debt free journey aesthetic | Inspirational | 4 | 5 | 5 | 2 | 16 | Validated (Guided Search) |
| debt payoff planner template | Commercial investigation | 5 | 4 | 3 | 5 | 17 | Validated (autocomplete) |
| paying off debt motivation | Inspirational | 3 | 5 | 5 | 1 | 14 | Brainstormed, not yet checked |

**Hand-off note:** the top three validated keywords route directly into pin titles and board names; "paying off debt motivation" needs an autocomplete check before it's used anywhere.

## Example 2: Single blog post, focus + secondary keywords

**Context:** A productivity blogger is writing one post about time blocking and needs a focus keyword plus secondary keywords before drafting.

**Steps taken:**
1. Seed: "time blocking"
2. Ran seed through Google autocomplete and Ahrefs' free Keyword Generator
3. Tagged intent: this topic is mostly informational
4. Selected one focus keyword and five secondary keywords based on relevance and realistic winnability for a mid-authority blog

**Output:**

- **Focus keyword:** "how to time block your day" (informational, illustrative KD 14, illustrative volume 2,400/mo)
- **Secondary keywords:** "time blocking method," "time blocking for adhd," "time blocking template," "time blocking vs to do list," "time blocking apps"
- **Placement plan:** focus keyword in title tag, H1, meta description, URL slug, first 100 words; secondary keywords distributed across H2s

**Hand-off note:** "time blocking for adhd" scored highest on business potential despite lower volume, since it matches a named lead magnet on the site. Recommended as the H2 immediately after the introduction, not buried.

## Example 3: Etsy listing title and tags

**Context:** A seller is listing a printable wedding seating chart template and needs a title and 13 tags optimized for Etsy search.

**Steps taken:**
1. Seed: "wedding seating chart"
2. Ran seed through Etsy's own search bar autocomplete (Etsy's autocomplete reflects its own search index, which differs from Google's)
3. Pulled additional variants from 3 competing top-ranking listings' titles
4. Tagged intent: this is entirely transactional, since Etsy search itself is a purchase-intent platform
5. Selected matching terms for the title (Etsy's title field is also a ranking signal) and a mix of matching and related terms for the 13 available tags

**Output:**

- **Title:** "Wedding Seating Chart Template, Printable Seating Display, Editable Table Plan, Wedding Reception Sign, Digital Download"
- **Tags (13):** wedding seating chart, seating chart template, printable seating chart, wedding table plan, reception seating sign, editable seating chart, digital wedding sign, wedding reception decor, seating display template, table assignment chart, wedding printable, wedding planning template, find your seat sign
- **Flag:** "find your seat sign" was brainstormed from a competitor title, not yet confirmed in Etsy's own autocomplete. Worth a quick check before finalizing.

## Example 4: AEO query fan-out for a FAQ page

**Context:** A SaaS company selling project management software wants its "what is a kanban board" page to get cited by AI search tools, not just rank traditionally.

**Steps taken:**
1. Core question: "what is a kanban board"
2. Mapped to funnel stage: primarily TOFU, with some MOFU crossover ("kanban board vs scrum board")
3. Brainstormed likely fan-out sub-queries: definition, history, how to use one, tools that offer one, comparison to alternatives
4. Validated two of the five brainstormed sub-queries by running the parent query across ChatGPT and Google AI Mode directly and noting the follow-up questions each one surfaced
5. Ranked the validated list

**Output:**

| Sub-query | Source | Suggested content block |
|---|---|---|
| what is a kanban board | Original query | 40-60 word direct-answer block, first 150 words |
| kanban board vs scrum board | Confirmed via AI Mode follow-up | Dedicated H2 with comparison table |
| how to make a kanban board | Confirmed via ChatGPT follow-up | H2 with numbered steps |
| best kanban board software | Brainstormed, not yet validated | Flagged; needs autocomplete check before becoming an H2 |
| history of the kanban method | Brainstormed, not yet validated | Flagged; lower priority, optional FAQ entry only |

**Hand-off note:** the direct-answer block for the core question should land in the first 150 words per the citability research in `references/aeo-ai-search-keywords.md` Section 5.

## Example 5: YouTube title and description keywords

**Context:** A home cooking channel is publishing a video on air fryer chicken thighs and wants a title and description optimized for YouTube search.

**Steps taken:**
1. Seed: "air fryer chicken thighs"
2. Ran seed through YouTube's own search bar autocomplete
3. Checked Google Trends for seasonal interest pattern (relevant since air fryer content spikes around certain times of year)
4. Tagged intent: informational/transactional mix (people want the result, not just the explanation)

**Output:**

- **Title:** "Crispy Air Fryer Chicken Thighs (Juicy Inside, 20 Minutes)"
- **Description opening line:** includes "air fryer chicken thighs recipe" and "crispy air fryer chicken thighs" within the first two sentences, since YouTube weighs early-description keywords more heavily
- **Secondary terms for tags/description:** "air fryer chicken thighs boneless," "air fryer chicken thighs frozen," "easy air fryer dinner"

**Flag:** "air fryer chicken thighs frozen" showed up in autocomplete but wasn't part of the original seed brainstorm; added after validation, a good example of autocomplete surfacing something the initial list missed.

## Example 6: Content calendar / cluster map for a content pillar

**Context:** A fitness creator is planning a quarter of blog content around "home workouts" and needs a full cluster map, not just one post.

**Steps taken:**
1. Macro research mode (see `references/blog-website-seo-keywords.md` Section 1)
2. Seed: "home workouts"
3. Expanded into related terms and sub-topics via Ahrefs' free Keyword Generator and manual competitor review
4. Clustered into parent topics so posts interlink instead of compete
5. Scored every candidate keyword and slotted the highest-ranked ones into a 12-week calendar

**Output (partial cluster map):**

- **Parent topic: "no equipment workouts"** → posts: "30 minute no equipment workout," "no equipment leg workout," "no equipment ab workout"
- **Parent topic: "home workout plans"** → posts: "beginner home workout plan," "home workout plan for weight loss," "4 week home workout plan"
- **Parent topic: "home workout equipment"** → posts: "best resistance bands for home workouts," "minimal equipment home gym essentials"

**Hand-off note:** "home workout plan for weight loss" scored highest on business potential (links directly to a paid program) and was scheduled for week 2, not buried later in the quarter.

## Example 7: Competitor gap analysis for a blog

**Context:** A personal finance blog wants to know what its top three competitors cover on "high yield savings accounts" that its own existing post doesn't.

**Steps taken:**
1. Pulled the top 5 ranking pages for "high yield savings accounts"
2. Listed every H2/H3 each of the 5 pages used
3. Compared that list against the site's own existing post outline
4. Flagged every subtopic present in competitor content but absent from the existing post

**Output:**

- **Gaps found:** "how compound interest works in a HYSA" (covered by 4 of 5 competitors, missing from the existing post), "HYSA vs CD comparison" (covered by 3 of 5, missing), "FDIC insurance limits" (covered by all 5, missing)
- **Not a gap:** "best high yield savings accounts 2026" (already covered)

**Hand-off note:** "FDIC insurance limits" is a small addition but was covered by every single competitor, which makes it a near-certain expectation gap rather than a nice-to-have.

## Example 8: Email subject line keyword optimization

**Context:** A newsletter writer wants two subject line options for an email announcing a new free template, optimized for open-worthy phrasing rather than search volume.

**Steps taken:**
1. Seed: the template's core benefit ("save time planning content")
2. Note: email subject lines aren't searched, so traditional search-volume tools don't apply here. Instead, the research pulls from the same customer-language principle (Section 1 of `references/core-keyword-fundamentals.md`) applied to phrases subscribers have actually used in replies or surveys
3. Pulled two phrases directly from past subscriber replies that described the same pain point in their own words
4. Drafted two subject line variants using that language instead of internal product naming

**Output:**

- **Option A:** "the thing that finally stopped my sunday-night content panic"
- **Option B:** "a free template for the planning part nobody likes"

**Note on methodology:** this example shows the skill's customer-language principle applied outside a traditional search context. There's no volume/difficulty data to validate against here; the "validation" step becomes A/B testing the two subject lines directly, since that's the closest equivalent to real-world confirmation for this channel.

## Example 9: Edge case, no paid tool available, brainstorm-only research

**Context:** A hobbyist blogger writing about home sourdough baking has no budget for paid tools and needs keyword research for a new post using only free resources.

**Steps taken:**
1. Seed generated from the post topic in plain language
2. Expanded using only free sources: Google autocomplete, Google Trends, and AnswerThePublic's free tier (3 searches/day cap)
3. No paid tool was available to cross-check volume or difficulty numbers
4. Every keyword in the final list was explicitly flagged according to its actual validation status, since none of them could be checked against a paid tool's volume/difficulty data

**Output:**

| Keyword | Intent | Validation status |
|---|---|---|
| sourdough starter not bubbling | Informational | Confirmed via Google autocomplete |
| why is my sourdough starter flat | Informational | Confirmed via AnswerThePublic |
| best flour for sourdough starter | Commercial investigation | Brainstormed only, not checked against any source |

**Why this matters as an example:** this is the correct way to handle a zero-budget research pass. The absence of a paid tool is not a reason to present brainstormed keywords with false confidence. Every keyword is still tagged with exactly how it was or wasn't validated, and the brainstormed-only keyword is clearly marked rather than blended in as if it had been checked. A reader using this output knows precisely which keywords to trust without further checking and which ones still need it.
